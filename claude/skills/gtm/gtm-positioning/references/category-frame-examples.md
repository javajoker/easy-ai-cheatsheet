# Category Frame Examples

The "category" in a positioning brief is the **market frame**
customers use to understand the product. Wrong category = wrong
mental model + wrong comparison set + wrong buyer.

## Three category strategies

### Strategy 1 — Match an existing category

Product fits cleanly within a well-known category; positioning
fights for share within it.

**Examples:**

| Product | Category | Why this frame |
|---|---|---|
| Stripe | Online payments | Existing category (PayPal, Braintree); fights on dev-friendliness |
| Datadog | Infrastructure monitoring | Existing category (New Relic, Splunk); fights on integrations |
| Figma | Design tools | Existing category (Sketch, Adobe XD); fights on collaboration |
| Notion | Productivity / wiki | Existing category (Confluence, Evernote); fights on flexibility |

**When to use.** Customers already know the category; you have
clear differentiation within it.

**Risks.** Comparison-shopping is brutal; you must clearly win
on at least one dimension.

---

### Strategy 2 — Re-frame an existing category

Product is in a known category but positioned to be considered a
different kind of thing.

**Examples:**

| Product | Apparent category | Re-frame to | Why |
|---|---|---|---|
| Slack | "Chat tool" | "Where work happens" | Broader than chat; replaces email for team comms |
| Salesforce | "CRM" | "Customer success platform" | Broader; not just sales record-keeping |
| Tesla | "Electric car" | "Software-defined car" | Distinguishes from compliance EVs (Bolt, Leaf) |
| Notion (later) | "Wiki" | "All-in-one workspace" | Beyond wiki; docs + tasks + DBs |

**When to use.** Customers misclassify the product when they
encounter it; the misclassification puts them in the wrong
comparison set.

**Risks.** Re-frame is hard; customers need to be re-educated;
sales cycle longer.

---

### Strategy 3 — Create a new category

Product is genuinely a new thing; no existing category fits well.

**Examples:**

| Product | New category | Why created |
|---|---|---|
| Salesforce (originally) | "No software" / "CRM SaaS" | Software-as-service was novel in 1999 |
| Drift | "Conversational marketing" | Live chat + automation + sales workflow combined |
| Slack (originally) | "Channel-based team communication" | Distinct from chat + email + project mgmt |
| HubSpot | "Inbound marketing" | Coined category to define the SEO/content era |

**When to use.** Genuine net-new behaviour; existing categories
actively mislead customers.

**Risks.** Massive education cost; if the category doesn't catch
on, the product is uncategorised + invisible. Only attempt with
significant marketing budget + clear product clarity.

---

## How to pick the category

Walk this decision tree:

```
Can customers find a clear existing category that matches?
├── YES, and we win on a clear dimension within it
│   → Strategy 1 (match)
├── YES, but the existing category misframes us
│   ├── We have marketing budget + sales-enablement time
│   │   → Strategy 2 (re-frame)
│   └── We don't
│       → Strategy 1 anyway; fight on differentiation
└── NO existing category fits well
    ├── We have significant resources + clarity
    │   → Strategy 3 (create)
    └── We don't
        → Strategy 2 (re-frame the nearest match)
```

## Category description in the brief

The brief's category section answers:

1. **What category are we in?** (one sentence)
2. **Who else is in this category?** (3–5 named competitors / adjacents)
3. **What's the customer's existing mental model?** (one paragraph)
4. **How do we want them to update that model?** (one paragraph)
5. **What proof points support the category claim?** (3–5 bullets)

### Example — Match strategy

> **Category:** Open-source observability platform.
>
> **Adjacents:** Datadog, New Relic, Honeycomb, Grafana Cloud.
>
> **Customer's mental model:** "Observability is expensive SaaS
> with vendor lock-in. The open-source alternatives are powerful
> but require ops investment."
>
> **Update:** "Open-source observability now matches SaaS on
> ease-of-use; we're the cloud-native bridge."
>
> **Proof points:**
>
> - OpenTelemetry-native (no proprietary agent).
> - Hosted offering for teams that want managed.
> - Self-hostable for teams with ops capacity.
> - Pricing 70% lower than equivalent SaaS at scale.

### Example — Re-frame strategy

> **Category we appear in:** Customer support helpdesk.
>
> **Category we want to be in:** Customer success platform.
>
> **Customer's mental model:** "Helpdesks are about ticket
> volume; success platforms are about retention. They're
> different teams + budgets."
>
> **Update:** "Tickets are the symptom; what matters is
> retention. Our 'support platform' integrates customer health,
> usage signals, and tickets into one workflow."
>
> **Proof points:**
>
> - NPS + health score embedded in ticket UI.
> - Usage data integrated; tickets show product context.
> - Retention dashboard for CS team alongside ticket queue.
> - Pricing model based on managed accounts, not seat count.

### Example — Create strategy

> **Category:** Continuous customer feedback automation.
>
> **Adjacents:** Survey tools (Typeform), session replay
> (FullStory), feedback widgets (Canny) — but each addresses
> a slice; we cover the loop.
>
> **Customer's mental model:** "Customer feedback is collected
> manually via surveys + tickets + reviews — siloed."
>
> **Update:** "Customer feedback should be a continuous,
> automated stream classified by AI, routed to the right team,
> with closed-loop response back to the customer."
>
> **Proof points:**
>
> - 200+ integrations to source feedback (Slack, support tools,
>   reviews, surveys, NPS).
> - LLM classification + sentiment scoring.
> - Auto-routed to product / engineering / CS teams.
> - Customer notified when their feedback ships.

## Anti-patterns

- **"We're a category of one."** Almost never true; usually
  reflects insufficient market research.
- **Picking a hot category to ride the wave.** Customers see
  through this; comparison-shopping reveals product mismatch.
- **No proof points.** Category claims without proof = empty
  positioning.
- **Re-framing without sales enablement.** Marketing says "we're
  X"; sales sells "we're Y"; customer is confused.
- **Creating a category mid-launch.** Category creation is a
  multi-year program. Plan accordingly or pick a different
  strategy.
